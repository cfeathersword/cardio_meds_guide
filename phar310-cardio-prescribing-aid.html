Conditions

Hypertension – stepwise approach

Applies to adult primary hypertension in the Australian setting; adjust for individual comorbidities, pregnancy and frailty.

  1. Confirm diagnosis – repeat BP on separate visits and exclude secondary causes (renal disease, primary aldosteronism, endocrine causes, sleep apnoea).
  2. Assess absolute CVD risk – use Aus CVD Risk Calculator; some groups are automatically high risk (e.g. CKD with eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73m², familial hypercholesterolaemia).
  3. Implement lifestyle measures for all – DASH-style diet, weight loss, sodium restriction, alcohol reduction, regular aerobic activity.
  4. Decide on drug therapy:
    • Start if ASCVD risk high (≥10%) or BP ≥160/100 mmHg regardless of risk.
    • Consider in intermediate risk (5–10%) or target organ damage.
  5. Choose first-line agent (uncomplicated HTN):
    First line
    • ACE inhibitor or ARB (sartan).
    • Dihydropyridine CCB (e.g. amlodipine, felodipine).
    • Low-dose thiazide/thiazide-like diuretic, especially ≥65 years.
  6. Tailor to comorbidities – see key combinations and contraindications below.
  7. If BP uncontrolled on maximally tolerated first agent:
    • Add a second drug from a complementary class rather than simply up-titrating one agent.
    • Preferred pairs: ACEi/ARB + CCB; ACEi/ARB + thiazide; CCB + thiazide.
  8. Resistant hypertension – if on ≥3 appropriate agents (including a diuretic) with good adherence and still uncontrolled:
    • Recheck adherence, white-coat effect, NSAIDs and other contributors.
    • Consider adding mineralocorticoid antagonist or other third-line drug under specialist guidance.

Drug classes – key points

ACE inhibitors (e.g. perindopril, ramipril, enalapril)

First line

Favour when: diabetes with albuminuria, CKD, HFrEF, post-MI, high ASCVD risk.

Contra / avoid: pregnancy, history of angioedema, bilateral renal artery stenosis, K⁺ >5 mmol/L, combination with ARB or aliskiren.

Watch for: rise in creatinine and K⁺ (check baseline and within 1–2 weeks), symptomatic hypotension, dry cough, rare angioedema.

ARBs (sartans – e.g. irbesartan, candesartan, valsartan)

First line

Use when: ACEi-indiced cough or intolerance; same indications as ACEi.

Contra / avoid: pregnancy, previous ARB-related angioedema, bilateral renal artery stenosis, combination with ACEi or potassium-sparing diuretics without close monitoring.

Watch for: hyperkalaemia, renal function decline, rare sprue-like enteropathy (especially with olmesartan).

Dihydropyridine CCBs (e.g. amlodipine, lercanidipine)

First line

Favour when: older patients, isolated systolic HTN, concomitant angina, asthma/COPD where β-blockers are problematic.

Caution: symptomatic aortic stenosis, severe hepatic impairment; may worsen peripheral oedema or cause reflex tachycardia.

Watch for: ankle swelling, flushing, headache, gingival hyperplasia; avoid strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g. azoles, clarithromycin, grapefruit).

Thiazide(-like) diuretics (e.g. hydrochlorothiazide, indapamide)

First line ≥65y

Favour when: older adults, volume-dependent HTN; can combine with ACEi/ARB.

Caution / avoid: gout, significant hyponatraemia, hypokalaemia, uncontrolled diabetes, severe renal impairment.

Watch for: Na⁺/K⁺, urate, glucose and lipids; counsel about dizziness and photosensitivity.

β-blockers

Second line

Use when: compelling indication – angina, post-MI, HFrEF (use HF-specific agents), rate control in AF.

Contra / avoid: uncontrolled asthma, severe COPD, bradycardia, second/third-degree AV block, decompensated HF.

Do not combine with verapamil (risk of severe bradycardia/heart block); use caution with diltiazem.

Hypertension – red flags

  • BP ≥180/110 mmHg with symptoms (headache, visual change, SOB, chest pain) – urgent assessment.
  • Pregnancy: avoid ACEi/ARB; use labetalol, nifedipine or methyldopa as per obstetric guidance.
  • Check for NSAIDs, OTC decongestants and oral contraceptives contributing to poor control.

Heart failure – pharmacological core

Focus on HFrEF; HFpEF emphasises risk-factor control, diuretics for congestion and SGLT2 inhibitors.

  1. Confirm type – echocardiogram to distinguish HFrEF (LVEF <50%, esp. ≤40%) from HFpEF (LVEF ≥50%).
  2. Stabilise congestion – loop diuretic (e.g. furosemide) to relieve pulmonary and peripheral oedema; titrate to least dose that controls symptoms.
  3. Start guideline-directed quadruple therapy for HFrEF (unless contraindicated):
    Essential core
    • RAAS inhibitor – ACEi, ARB or ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan).
    • HF-specific β-blocker – bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol CR, nebivolol.
    • Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist – spironolactone or eplerenone.
    • SGLT2 inhibitor – empagliflozin or dapagliflozin (benefit with or without diabetes).
  4. Introduce agents early – start low doses of all four as soon as clinically feasible, then up-titrate every 2–4 weeks as tolerated.
  5. Add-on options for persistent symptoms – ivabradine (sinus rhythm, HR ≥70 on max β-blocker), digoxin, vericiguat or device therapy under specialist care.

Key drug cautions

RAAS inhibition (ACEi, ARB, ARNI)

Avoid dual blockade – do not combine ACEi + ARB or add aliskiren.

Contra / avoid: pregnancy, history of angioedema, bilateral renal artery stenosis, K⁺ >5 mmol/L, severe hypotension.

Switching to ARNI: allow 36 h washout after last ACEi dose to reduce angioedema risk.

Monitor: BP, renal function and K⁺ at baseline and after each titration; watch for symptomatic hypotension and worsening renal function.

MRA (spironolactone, eplerenone)

Benefits: reduced mortality and HF hospitalisations in HFrEF.

Contra / avoid: K⁺ ≥5 mmol/L at baseline, severe renal impairment (e.g. eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m²), Addison disease.

Dose hints: in HFrEF, start spironolactone 12.5–25 mg once daily or eplerenone 25 mg once daily and titrate to 25–50 mg daily if K⁺ and renal function allow; use reduced starting doses when eGFR 30–49 mL/min/1.73m² and avoid initiating if eGFR <30.

Monitor: K⁺ and creatinine at baseline, 1–2 weeks after initiation and dose changes, then every 3–6 months; counsel to avoid potassium supplements and salt substitutes.

HF β-blockers

Do not start during acute decompensated HF or in patients with fluid overload.

Initiation: very low dose with slow up-titration; transient worsening of HF can occur.

Contra / avoid: severe bradycardia, advanced AV block without pacemaker, uncontrolled asthma, cardiogenic shock.

Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide)

Use for: symptom control of congestion; aim for stable dry weight.

Monitor: Na⁺, K⁺, Mg²⁺, creatinine, weight and blood pressure.

Caution: gout, hypovolaemia, significant hypotension, higher doses in CKD; risk of ototoxicity with high IV doses or combination with other ototoxic drugs.

HFpEF considerations

  • Use SGLT2 inhibitor for all suitable HFpEF patients (reduced CV death/HF admission).
  • Treat precipitating factors (hypertension, AF, ischaemia, obesity, OSA).
  • Avoid routine vasodilators that may worsen symptoms (e.g. high-dose nitrates, hydralazine) unless clear indication.

Dyslipidaemia – treatment ladder

Therapy is based on absolute ASCVD risk and LDL level; lifestyle measures underpin all pharmacotherapy.

  1. Assess ASCVD risk using Aus CVD Risk Calculator and identify automatically high-risk groups (e.g. CKD, familial hypercholesterolaemia).
  2. Initiate lifestyle modification – Mediterranean-style diet, reduced saturated and trans fats, weight loss, increased activity, alcohol reduction, smoking cessation.
  3. Start statin when indicated by risk or LDL (e.g. total cholesterol >7.5 mmol/L or LDL >5 mmol/L irrespective of risk).
    First line
  4. Recheck lipids ~6 weeks after initiation or dose change; compare to LDL, non-HDL and triglyceride targets by risk category.
  5. If LDL not at target on maximally tolerated statin:
    • Add ezetimibe.
    • If still above target and meets criteria, add PCSK9 inhibitor (evolocumab or inclisiran).
  6. If triglycerides remain high despite statin and lifestyle:
    • Add high-dose omega-3 (2–4 g EPA+DHA per day);
    • Consider fenofibrate when triglycerides >10 mmol/L or persistent elevation.

Key drugs and cautions

Statins

First line

High potency: atorvastatin, rosuvastatin (preferred post-ACS and in very high risk).

Watch for: myalgia, myopathy, raised CK, new-onset diabetes, liver enzyme elevation.

Caution / avoid: active liver disease, pregnancy and breastfeeding; avoid strong CYP3A4 inhibitors with atorvastatin/simvastatin and excessive grapefruit juice.

Ezetimibe

Add-on

Use with statin when LDL target not achieved; alternative if statin intolerance.

Watch for: muscle symptoms, elevated liver enzymes (especially with statin), rare hypersensitivity.

PCSK9 inhibitors (evolocumab, inclisiran)

Add-on (specialist)

Strict authority criteria (familial hypercholesterolaemia or established ASCVD with high LDL despite maximal therapy).

Watch for: injection-site reactions; inclisiran may cause respiratory infections.

Fibrates (fenofibrate, gemfibrozil)

TG-focused

Use mainly for severe hypertriglyceridaemia and residual high TG despite statin ± fish oil.

Dose hints: fenofibrate 145 mg once daily (use lower doses and careful monitoring when CrCl 30–59 mL/min); gemfibrozil 600 mg twice daily; avoid fibrates when CrCl <30 mL/min.

Contra / avoid: CrCl <30 mL/min, significant hepatic impairment, history of fibrate-induced photosensitivity or gallstones.

Watch for: GI upset, myopathy (especially with statin), photosensitivity; advise sun protection.

Bile-acid resin (colestyramine) & nicotinic acid

Second line

Use when first-line options insufficient or contraindicated; often limited by tolerability and interactions.

Watch for: significant GI effects and reduced absorption of other drugs and fat-soluble vitamins with colestyramine; flushing, hepatotoxicity and hyperglycaemia with nicotinic acid.

Special situations

  • Pregnancy: avoid statins, ezetimibe, fibrates and nicotinic acid; bile-acid resins may be used with caution and specialist input.
  • Combination with other myotoxic drugs (e.g. some antivirals, azoles, fibrates): check for interactions and consider lower statin doses or alternatives.

Stable angina – chronic management

Unstable angina and NSTEACS should follow ACS pathways. This section covers chronic stable angina.

  1. Confirm diagnosis – typical exertional chest pain relieved by rest or GTN, supported by stress testing/imaging.
  2. Optimise secondary prevention – antiplatelet (usually aspirin), statin, BP control, diabetes and lifestyle risk-factor modification.
  3. Provide short-acting GTN to all patients for symptom relief and pre-exertional prophylaxis.
  4. First-line long-term antianginal:
    First line
    • β-blocker (e.g. metoprolol, bisoprolol) – preferred, especially with prior MI or HFrEF.
    • Non-dihydropyridine CCB (verapamil, diltiazem) if β-blockers contraindicated.
  5. If symptoms persist – add dihydropyridine CCB or long-acting nitrate; if still refractory, consider nicorandil, ivabradine or perhexiline with specialist advice.
  6. Vasospastic (Prinzmetal) angina – use CCBs or long-acting nitrates; avoid β-blockers which can worsen spasm.

Nitrates and other antianginals – safety

Short-acting GTN (sublingual spray)

Use: 1–2 sprays under tongue at onset; repeat every 5 minutes up to 3 doses. Call ambulance if pain >10 minutes despite 2 doses.

Absolute contraindication: PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil, vardenafil, avanafil) within prior 24–48 h – risk of profound hypotension and MI.

Counsel: sit before dosing to avoid syncope; expect headache and flushing; ensure device is primed and not expired.

Long-acting nitrates (isosorbide mononitrate, GTN patch)

Use: prophylaxis when frequent angina episodes; must include daily nitrate-free interval (e.g. remove patch for 8–12 h or once-daily morning dosing) to prevent tolerance.

Watch for: orthostatic hypotension, headache, ankle swelling.

Nicorandil

Option for refractory angina added to β-blocker or CCB.

Caution / avoid: combination with PDE5 inhibitors; diverticular disease or history of GI/genital ulcers or fistulae.

Counsel: report any non-healing oral, perianal, GI or skin ulcers promptly.

Ivabradine & perhexiline

Reserved for patients not controlled with, or intolerant of, first-line agents; require specialist initiation and careful monitoring (esp. perhexiline levels and hepatotoxicity).

Acute coronary syndrome – high-level pharmacotherapy

Summarises core drug classes; acute management must follow local ACS protocols and cardiology advice.

  1. Immediate measures (pre-hospital or on arrival) – aspirin 300 mg chewed, sublingual GTN for pain, opioid if needed, oxygen only if SpO₂ <90%.
  2. Classify ACS – STEMI vs NSTEACS using ECG and high-sensitivity troponin; initiate continuous ECG monitoring.
  3. Reperfusion in STEMI – primary PCI within 120 min preferred; otherwise fibrinolysis (e.g. tenecteplase) plus transfer for angiography.
  4. Antithrombotic backbone for all ACS (unless contraindicated):
    • Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT): aspirin + P2Y₁₂ inhibitor (ticagrelor or prasugrel preferred; clopidogrel if others unsuitable).
    • Parenteral anticoagulant: UFH or LMWH; bivalirudin in selected high-bleeding-risk PCI cases.
  5. NSTEACS – risk-stratify (e.g. GRACE score) to determine early invasive vs conservative strategy; fibrinolysis is not used.
  6. Secondary prevention on discharge – DAPT for 6–12 months, then single antiplatelet; high-intensity statin; ACEi/ARB; β-blocker if LV dysfunction or other indication; MRA in selected HFrEF; vaccinations and cardiac rehab.

Major contraindications / cautions

  • Fibrinolysis: prior intracranial haemorrhage, ischaemic stroke within 3 months, intracranial neoplasm or vascular lesion, active bleeding, recent major head trauma.
  • Prasugrel: not for patients not undergoing PCI; avoid in prior stroke/TIA and in very elderly/high bleeding risk.
  • DAPT plus anticoagulation (e.g. in AF): minimise duration of triple therapy; favour DOAC + single antiplatelet once early period has passed.

Atrial fibrillation – rate, rhythm, stroke prevention

Always treat underlying precipitants (thyroid disease, infection, alcohol, proarrhythmic drugs) before escalating antiarrhythmics.

1. Rate vs rhythm control

  • Rate control (often first choice): β-blocker, diltiazem or verapamil; digoxin in sedentary patients or as add-on.
  • Rhythm control: electrical cardioversion or antiarrhythmic drugs (e.g. amiodarone, flecainide, sotalol) under specialist guidance.

Rate control – key cautions

Do not combine: β-blocker + verapamil (risk of severe bradycardia/AV block).

β-blockers: avoid in uncontrolled asthma, severe COPD, marked bradycardia or high-grade AV block.

Non-DHP CCBs: avoid in HFrEF and significant bradycardia.

Digoxin: narrow therapeutic index; avoid in second/third-degree AV block without pacemaker, WPW with AF, hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy and significant renal impairment without dose adjustment.

Rhythm control – QT and proarrhythmia

Amiodarone, sotalol and other class III agents prolong QT and can provoke torsades de pointes, particularly with other QT-prolonging drugs or electrolyte disturbance.

Check: baseline QTc, K⁺, Mg²⁺, renal function and co-medications (e.g. macrolides, azole antifungals, some antipsychotics and antidepressants).

Flecainide: avoid in structural heart disease or ischaemic cardiomyopathy; use with AV nodal blocker to prevent 1:1 atrial flutter conduction.

2. Stroke prevention in AF

  • Estimate stroke risk (e.g. CHA₂DS₂-VA score); oral anticoagulant generally recommended if score ≥2.
  • Oral anticoagulant options: DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran) usually preferred; warfarin for rheumatic mitral stenosis, mechanical valves or severe CKD.

CHA₂DS₂-VA helper (AF stroke risk estimation)

Score components: CHF (1), HTN (1), Age 75+ (2), Age 65–74 (1), Diabetes (1), Stroke/TIA/SE (2), Vascular disease (1).

DOACs

Do not use in mechanical valves or rheumatic mitral stenosis – warfarin only.

Dose hints (stroke prevention in AF): apixaban 5 mg twice daily (2.5 mg twice daily if ≥2 of age ≥80 years, weight ≤60 kg, creatinine ≥133 micromol/L); rivaroxaban 20 mg once daily with food (15 mg once daily if CrCl 15–49 mL/min); dabigatran 150 mg twice daily, with lower doses for older or higher bleeding-risk patients according to local guidance.

Renal function: review CrCl regularly and reduce dose or avoid if below thresholds for each agent; for example, avoid dabigatran if CrCl <30 mL/min, rivaroxaban if CrCl <15 mL/min, and consider alternative strategies when severe CKD is present.

Interactions: strong CYP3A4 and/or P-gp inhibitors or inducers (e.g. some azoles, HIV therapies, rifampicin) can markedly alter effect.

Warfarin

Target INR: usually 2.0–3.0 (higher for some prosthetic valves).

Monitor: frequent INR checks when initiating or changing interacting medications; counsel regarding consistent vitamin K intake.

Interactions: many drugs (e.g. antibiotics, azoles, amiodarone) and herbal products (e.g. St John’s wort) plus NSAIDs which increase bleeding risk.

Renal function & prescribing checklist

Use eGFR for staging CKD and creatinine clearance (CrCl) or local dosing guidelines for drug adjustment.

1. Assess kidney function

  • Check latest eGFR and trend; distinguish AKI from stable CKD.
  • Estimate CrCl (e.g. Cockcroft–Gault) when dosing drugs that reference CrCl.
  • In extremes of body size, very low or high muscle mass, or rapidly changing renal function, values may be unreliable – use measured CrCl or specialist advice.

2. Identify high-risk drugs

  • Renally cleared with narrow therapeutic index (e.g. digoxin, some antiarrhythmics, DOACs, aminoglycosides, vancomycin).
  • Nephrotoxic agents (e.g. NSAIDs, contrast media, some chemotherapy, high-dose diuretics).
  • Combinations that precipitate AKI – classic triple whammy: NSAID + ACEi/ARB + diuretic.

3. Adjust and monitor

  • Modify dose or dosing interval as per AMH/eTG or local protocol.
  • Repeat renal function after starting or increasing doses of ACEi/ARB, MRA, SGLT2 inhibitor or diuretics.
  • For AKI, hold nephrotoxic and renally cleared drugs where appropriate until recovery.
